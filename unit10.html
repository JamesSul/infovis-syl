Lecture Notes

First off, quick reminder that the quiz 5 is due at the end of this unit and has material from all the units we've covered since the last quiz.

This unit is setup somewhat different from the units we have seen to date. There is no video that I produced -- you will have to suffer a full week without listening to my dulcet voice. There is a substantial amount more video to watch than any prior week: 1 hours, 46 minutes, 33 seconds to be exact. I'm describing them below in the order I would like you to view them. Note: I include their length in h:m:s format.

The first video is Jonathan Corum: Storytelling with data (35:47). Corum is the science graphics editor at The New York Times and is presenting at the May, 2013 Tapestry Conference which is a conference dedicated to storytelling with data. In chapter 13, Few describes the value of being familiar with your audience. Corum really gives you a sense of what this means.

The next video I want you to watch is Michael Fink, Yinnon Haviv, Dani Bacon: Data Driven Storytelling (53:03) from Google I/O 2012. It exposes you to Google Charts for both exploration and storytelling. It is a little code-heavy so I recommend you watch the following sections: (0:00 - 10:00, 19:48 - 30:30, 38:30 - end). Warning: you will see a lot of donut charts here. The last section exposes you to a data analysis category we really don't discuss in this class, the quantified self, which is going to be a great source of data analysis and visualization for the foreseeable future (think fitbit).

The next video is a tutorial introducing Tableau Story Points (06:09). You'll probabaly want to consider adding story points to your final project. Note: you will need to login to access this and I can't remember if your Tableau public account will work or you need to create a new customer account (I have the same account information for both). If you can't login with your public account then just create a free customer account.

One of the items that Few tends to take issue with is embellishments and "cool things." Not surprisingly, using technology to accomplish this is absent from chapter 13. Joe Sabia: The technology of storytelling (3:51) is a TED presentation that will make you never forget the name Lothar Meggendorfer. Sabia is a NYC based remix artist.

Finally, with all the dynamism that an economist can muster, Tyler Cowen, in his TED talk, warns us to Be suspicious of stories (16:31). Cowen, an economics professor at George Mason University, reinforces that stories are simple, easy to grasp, and easy to remember. He contends that this tends to make them inaccurate.
