Due: Assignment 3

This assignment incorporates at least two data files. The first file is data on NCAA average coaches salaries. It has 2090 records. The second file contains revenues and expenses for 872 athletic programs for colleges and universities that have football. Your assignment is straightforward:

  • Show me something interesting.
  • You must use data from both files (i.e., something related to revenues and/or expenses AND something related to salaries).
  • You may incorporate other data you find from other sources.
  • If what you find interesting uses only a subset of the data (e.g., NCAA Division II) that is fine.
  • In the narrative, describe what you find interesting about your visualization, what visual encodings you are using, and why.
  • Your narrative can be in your Piazza post or directly on your visualization on the Tableau Public Server.
  • Remember to post a link to your visualization as a note in Piazza.

Lecture Notes

Small Multiples

If there is one graph type I want you to remember Chapter 11 (displaying many variables at once) it is the small multiple -- also called the trellis, lattice, grid, or panel chart. It was popularized by Edward Tufte in the 1980s. In the video, I use Tableau to show how to create small multiples but let me attempt to explain why in these notes. The chart below shows seasonally adjusted unemployment rates for select countries for several months. We have the following variables: unemployment rate - y axis, month - x axis, country - color and label. I used the line to encode my x and y points as this is time series data.

If I want to break this out by gender, I have the potential to quickly lose reader comprehension. I can use shading, color, or some other encodings for gender but they will all have some shortcomings. Below I use additional colors.

I can actually handle two additional variables by using small multiples but I'll show you one way to handle the addition of gender. I make smaller multiple graphs for each country and then use color to encode gender as shown below.

You can also do small multiples in Excel but it isn't necessarily as intuitive as Tableau.

Silly Graphs

Few is clearly in the Tufte camp of always using the most appropriate graphical form to communicate your message, where appropriate uses the preattentive and graphical concepts we have covered to date. I can't argue with his premise that all of the charts in Chapter 11 (silly graphs) are less comprehensible than alternative choices. I can, however, point to examples of storytelling where there might be a tradeoff between comprehension and engaging your reader. This is most prevalent in journalism.

One of the silly graphs Few points to is the stacked area graph. There is a beautiful example of a stacked area graph showing net box office receipts for moves produced by the New York Times. The static image is below but I encourage you to click this link and interact with the dynamic graph.

There was even a paper that used this graphic to discuss the tradeoff between reader engagement and comprehension published in IEEE Transactions on Visualization and Computer Graphics (2008) written by Lee Byron, then graphics editor at the NY Times and Martin Wattenberg, who ran IBM's visual communications lab.

Another silly graph is the donut chart. All of the horrible aspects of the pie chart PLUS you lose the preattentive comparator of the pie chart angles. Even though this is a less readable chart, it is consistently used by The Economist magazine to engage readers (see example below).

Finally, Few lambastes unit charts like the one shown below. Yet the most viral visualization video to date examining income inequality utilizes this exact chart in a compelling manner. I encourage you to watch the entire video.

If you are creating visualizations for corporate reports, Few's advice is golden and you should have good reason to violate his recommendations. If you are in marketing or communications you'll need to weigh reader engagement more heavily than your corporate reporting counterparts.

In the final video for this unit, I cover an important concept that you'll need to understand for the assignment due this week. Merging files. If you have done relational database table joins, it is a simple concept. The video presents a single way to perform a merger (after cleaning and transforming). Keep in mind that there are many tools to accomplish this but I'm using Tableau for the merge, OpenRefine for the cleaning and Excel to place the two files in a single workbook.
